March 22, 2026 23-minute read

Thomas Piketty’s Capital in the Twenty-First Century emphasizes that the explosion of inequality in recent decades has been driven not by impersonal market forces alone but by an unprecedented surge in top managers’ earnings. Piketty documents that in the U.S. roughly 60–70% of incomes in the top 0.1% (2000–2010) came from “supermanagers,” far outpacing the share from entertainers or athletes[1]. He attributes this to corporate governance failures: today’s top executives effectively set their own pay. “These top managers by and large have the power to set their own remuneration, in some cases without limit,” Piketty observes, often with little relation to productivity[2][3]. In other words, much of the “gap” driving high-end inequality is rent extracted by managerial elites.

Empirical evidence confirms this insight. Over the past four decades, CEO pay in the U.S. has skyrocketed (EPI data show an inflation-adjusted 1,322% rise in realized CEO compensation from 1978–2020[4]) far outpacing both stock market growth and gains of other high earners. By 2024 the average CEO at a top 350 firm earned over 280× a typical worker’s pay[5] (see chart below). Studies find pay rises are strongly tied to peer-group benchmarking: boards often target or exceed the 50th percentile of competitor pay[6], which systematically ratchets up compensation over time. As Bebchuk and colleagues note, in the U.S. the power structure (highly dispersed ownership, CEOs with little outside stakes) makes rent extraction common[7].

Real-world cases vividly illustrate self-awarded pay and governance breakdowns. For example, Wells Fargo’s fake-accounts scandal (2016) saw its CEO John Stumpf collect roughly $130 million on exit even as regulators fined him $20m[8]. WeWork’s founder Adam Neumann was ousted in 2019 after a failed IPO, yet in 2021 still secured an estimated $445 million exit package (including cash, stock, and debt relief) from SoftBank[9]. In the UK, Royal Bank of Scotland (RBS) shareholders revolted in 2009: 90% voted down the pay report over Sir Fred Goodwin’s £703k/yr pension after a taxpayer bailout[10]. These and other cases (Enron’s 2001 collapse, Uber’s governance crisis, shareholder uprisings at European firms like InterContinental Hotels[11]) underscore how boards and compensation committees have often failed to check excess pay.

Statistical trends reinforce the connection between governance and inequality. (Chart: U.S. CEO-to-worker pay ratio, 1965–2024
.) Here, top CEO pay soared from ~21× a worker’s pay in 1965 to ~366× in 2000 and still ~281× by 2024[5]. Similarly, U.S. top income shares have swung from ~45–50% (1910–20) down to ~30% mid-century and back up to ~45% by the 2000s[12], driven largely by labor (especially top managerial) earnings[13]. Cross-country data also show that weak shareholder rights and board oversight correlate with higher pay ratios.

Underlying mechanisms include compensation committees’ use of peer benchmarking, the influence of pay consultants, and weak oversight by boards. In effect, CEO pay-setting becomes circular: boards reference peer pay (often larger firms with similarly expensive CEOs), pushing up the median; CEOs whose pay is below the median get outsized raises; this inflates peers and so on. As studies note, most firms set CEO pay at or above the peer-group median[6], exploiting “ratchet” effects. This dynamic resembles Piketty’s description of patrimonial tendencies: without strong constraints (low “outrage cost”), managers take rents.

Counterarguments – that the rise reflects skill-biased markets or globalization – have some merit but fall short. Gabaix and Landier (2008) model CEOs as “superstars,” noting that rising firm size (U.S. equity values grew ~6× from 1980s to 2000s) could justify roughly commensurate pay growth[14]. But Piketty (and EPI analysts) point out CEOs now earn far more than even other very top professionals – EPI finds CEO pay is over six times that of the top 0.1% average[4] – suggesting talent scarcity alone is insufficient. Moreover, Piketty stresses that the classic skill-supply model “does not explain the rise of the supermanager” after 1980[15]. “Talent-based” theories also fail to account for U.S.-UK outliers; if real productivity is unchanged, the surge must reflect power dynamics.

Policy Options: To curb self-awarded managerial rents, analysts recommend a mix of tax, disclosure, governance, and legal reforms. For example, reinstating higher marginal income taxes (e.g. new brackets on earnings above $1m, $5m, etc.) would directly reduce the after-tax gain from huge pay[16]. Targeted levies (“excess compensation” excise taxes) could impose penalties on firms whose CEO pay exceeds a threshold (e.g. $1 excise for each $1 above $1m of CEO salary[17]). Strengthening “say-on-pay” is another lever: unlike current advisory votes, proposals include making rejected pay packages trigger sanctions (e.g. directors lose fees or seats if shareholders vote them down)[18]. Requiring clawbacks for misconduct and making boards more independent can also help (e.g. reform board selection, ban dual-class shares). Some advocate hard limits like mandated CEO-to-worker pay ratio caps, but these are rare and can be gamed. Labor-market reforms – for instance bolstering union bargaining – are also key; unions tend to compress pay scales and check excesses.

Each policy has trade-offs. Higher tax rates may spur avoidance and capital flight. Disclosure rules (like SEC pay-ratio disclosures) improve transparency but don’t force change. Excise taxes and shareholder votes put pressure on boards, but effective design is crucial to avoid loopholes[19][18]. International coordination (to prevent firms shifting domicile) is also important, given the global nature of many corporations. In sum, a multifaceted approach – combining fiscal penalties, stronger governance norms, and labor empowerment – is needed to rein in self-interested executive pay and align it more closely with genuine value creation.

Piketty’s Perspective on Managers and Top Incomes Link to heading

Piketty’s central finding is that the rise in inequality is driven by labor (especially managerial) incomes, not capital owners. In his Figure I.1, the U.S. top decile’s share of national income fell from ~45–50% (1910s–20s) to ~30% mid-century, then “rapidly rose” back to ~45–50% by 2000[12]. Crucially, Piketty notes that this rebound “largely reflects an unprecedented explosion of very elevated incomes from labor, a veritable separation of the top managers of large firms from the rest of the population”[2]. In plain terms, it wasn’t wealthier capitalists driving today’s top incomes so much as highly paid corporate executives.

He emphasizes the power of these managers over pay-setting. Whereas economists often assume wages reflect marginal productivity, Piketty is skeptical: one “cannot simply assume that a worker’s wage equals his marginal product,” especially at the very top[20]. He observes that in practice social power plays a role in pay determination. In particular, “top managers by and large have the power to set their own remuneration” – often “without any clear relation to their individual productivity”[2][3]. In effect, top executives act like a class of insiders who negotiate among themselves and with board members (often peers or old colleagues) to keep upping pay.

For example, Piketty cites data showing that U.S. athletes and entertainers account for only ~5% of the top 0.1% incomes, whereas “supermanagers” dominate the rest[1]. This suggests the “superstar” literature (focused on isolated talents) misses that top corporate pay is the main driver. He even critiques the narrative that “talented managers have replaced fat-cat stockholders”[21], warning that capital still matters and managers often entrench their own pay. In short, Piketty’s text reads as a focused indictment of managerial rent extraction: the insiders who can lobby the board and use corporate rules to pad their salaries and bonuses, thereby fueling inequality.

Empirical Evidence on Executive Pay and Governance Link to heading

Numerous studies corroborate Piketty’s claim that governance weaknesses – not pure market forces – have driven executive pay. For U.S. firms, long-term trends are stark: using Compustat/ExecuComp data, the Economic Policy Institute finds that average CEO pay (realized) rose from ~$2 million in 1978 to ~$24.2 million in 2020[22] (an 18.9% jump in 2020 alone). From 1978–2020, realized CEO pay grew 1,322% (vs. a 341% rise for top 0.1% wages and only 18% for typical workers)[4]. By 2024, the typical S&P 350 CEO earned ~281× a front-line worker[5] (Chart above). These data underscore that CEO pay far outstripped other top incomes and market returns, implying a special force at work.

What are the forces? Research points to the role of boards and benchmarking. Bizjack, Lemmon, and Naveen (J. Accnt & Econ 2011) find that almost all large U.S. firms use peer groups to set pay[6]. Nearly every S&P 500 company they surveyed positions its CEO at or above the peer median, causing a “ratchet effect” that pushes pay higher over time. In fact, firms with CEOs below the median tend to give bigger raises than those already above the median[23]. This practice can inflate everyone’s pay without any productivity gains. In line with this, Frydman & Jenter (NBER 2010) and others report that pay is significantly correlated with peers’ pay[6], even after controlling for performance. Boards may claim benchmarking is “market-based,” but as Bizjack et al. concede, it often reflects retention worries more than true market signals[24].

Beyond benchmarking, managerial power is emphasized by governance scholars. Bebchuk, Fried, and Walker (2002) formulate a “managerial power” model: boards are often beholden to CEOs (socially or professionally), allowing entrenched executives to extract rents. They show that when power is unequally balanced, pay contracts deviate systematically from optimal incentives. Empirically, Bebchuk and colleagues note that in the U.S. (dispersed ownership context), “managers have considerable power” and “U.S. CEOs rarely have other business interests,” meaning few external constraints on pay[7]. In plain terms, with no large shareholder to discipline them and few competing roles to occupy, CEOs can lobby for bigger pay. Further evidence: studies find compensation consultants (hired by management) often recommend generous packages, and many board members sit on each other’s boards (“revolving doors”)[24][7], reducing independence.

At the same time, globalization and technology have uplifted all top incomes, including CEOs. Gabaix & Landier (2008) propose that CEOs are “superstars”: as firm value multiplies (e.g. U.S. equity markets grew ~6-fold from the 1980s), CEOs’ “productivity” (and thus pay) should scale similarly[14]. They argue the proliferation of MBAs means skill shortages are unlikely the main driver; instead, bigger firms bid up CEO pay. This model can explain some of the rise – e.g. tech giants and large multinationals need top talent – but it sidesteps Piketty’s point that governance distortion is crucial. Even if firm size grew 6×, CEO pay grew 30× or more, implying extra factors. In fact, Piketty explicitly observes that relying on skill-demand (education/technology) misses the supermanager jump in the 1980s–2000s[15]. On the empirical side, note that CEOs now out-earn other highly skilled workers by a much bigger margin: EPI finds CEOs earn over six times what the other 0.1% earn[4]. Pure skill-based or market arguments struggle to rationalize that gap without invoking power and rent.

Case Studies: Corporate Pay and Governance Fails Link to heading

The theory is borne out in high-profile cases. Below is a timeline of key episodes illustrating how executives enriched themselves amid governance lapses:

timeline
    title Major CEO Pay and Governance Cases
    2001 : **Enron (US):** Company collapses in fraud, $700m+ in top exec bonuses revealed. Executives Skilling/Lay jailed.
    2009 : **RBS (UK):** Shareholders revolt at AGM as former CEO Goodwin’s £703k/yr pension sparks 90% “against” vote[10].
    2016 : **Wells Fargo (US):** Fake account scandal; CEO Stumpf resigns but walks away with ~$130m compensation[8].
    2017 : **Uber (US):** Travis Kalanick ousted amid board/management turmoil; retains ~10% stake (worth ~$7B).
    2019 : **WeWork (US):** Adam Neumann forced out after failed IPO; subsequently given ~$445m exit package by SoftBank[9].
    2020 : **VW Dieselgate (DE):** CEO Winterkorn resigns after emissions scandal; no criminal charges despite €30B fines.
    2024 : **Europe-wide:** Institutional shareholders strike back – eg. IHG’s new pay policy won only 69.5% support in 2025[11].
  • Enron (2001, US): Energy trader Enron’s dramatic bankruptcy exposed wildly outsized executive pay. In the years before 2001, top Enron officers (CEO Lay, COO Skilling, CFO Fastow) received millions in stock awards and options. The collapse and subsequent Sarbanes-Oxley reforms highlighted that Enron’s board had rubber-stamped these packages despite mounting losses (a glaring governance failure).
  • Wells Fargo (2016, US): As recounted by whistleblower Kieran Cuadras[8], Wells’ executives aggressively set unattainable sales quotas for branch staff, spawning a fake-accounts crisis. CEO John Stumpf personally collected increasing bonuses even as malpractice grew. When regulators fined Wells Fargo $185 million, Stumpf had already walked off with roughly $130 million in pay and severance[8]. Neither he nor his board faced meaningful private-sector penalties, illustrating the ease of extracting pay before accountability.
  • WeWork (2019, US): Founder Adam Neumann’s charismatic leadership drove WeWork’s sky-high valuations. Yet internal weaknesses were ignored by the board. When the IPO attempt failed and Neumann was forced out, he nevertheless secured an enormous exit deal: initially reported at $1.7 billion by Bloomberg (including stock and debt benefits)[9]. A Guardian exposé details that Neumann got $245m in stock and $200m in cash from SoftBank, plus relief on $432m debt and permission to sell $578m of company shares[9]. Governance experts were stunned: “The captain rammed the ship into a bridge and then was given the value of the ship to leave,” quipped Charles Elson[25]. Meanwhile, over 90% of WeWork’s staff options became worthless.
  • Uber (2017, US): Travis Kalanick’s aggressive style led to fast growth but many ethics lapses (harassment, regulatory fights). A soft coup by investors forced his resignation. Nonetheless, Kalanick remained on the board with a huge equity stake (~10%), which Forbes later valued around $7 billion. This outcome underscores that even when U.S. boards act (at investor prodding), it can take years and result in minimal sacrifice for the ousted executive.
  • RBS (2009, UK): In the UK, the post-crisis backlash was vivid. Royal Bank of Scotland, 83% nationalized after the 2008 crash, faced shareholder fury over CEO Fred Goodwin’s retirement package. At the 2009 AGM a staggering 90% of investors voted against the directors’ pay report, outraged that Sir Fred still enjoyed a £703,000/year pension after overseeing massive losses[10]. (Goodwin ultimately gave up only £200k/yr of it under pressure.) The vote was symbolic – even a rebellion of that size couldn’t force full clawback – but it demonstrated popular anger at self-awarded pay.
  • European Shareholder Activism (2024–25): The struggle is global. For example, in 2025 Europe’s largest firms saw record opposition to pay policies. According to Reuters, nearly 38% of companies faced >10% “against” votes, up 23% from 2024[26]. British hotelier IHG reported only 69.5% support for its new remuneration policy at the 2025 AGM[11] (below the 80% “safe” threshold in UK corporate governance). In Italy, UniCredit drew one-third of votes against its pay plan[27]. These cases reflect growing investor and public scrutiny of top pay in well-regulated markets.

Each case highlights a common pattern: executives or boards engineered large payouts, even as companies floundered or engaged in malfeasance, and traditional oversight tools (shareholder votes, regulatory fines) were weak or slow. Piketty’s warning – that unchecked managerial pay can explode income concentration – finds concrete expression in these real-world stories.

Statistical Trends in Top Incomes and Pay Link to heading

*Chart: Ratio of CEO-to-typical-worker pay in the U.S., 1965–2024 (realized and granted measures). Data source: EPI[\[5\]](https://www.epi.org/blog/ceo-pay-increased-in-2024-and-is-now-281-times-that-of-the-typical-worker-new-epi-landing-page-has-all-the-details/#:~:text=2018%20284,57).*

This chart illustrates the stunning rise in executive pay relative to workers. Using realized compensation (which counts stock awards when vested), the CEO–worker pay gap widened from roughly 21:1 in 1965 to a peak of 366:1 in 2000[28], settling to about 280:1 by 2024[5]. (Under the “granted” measure, which counts stock at grant, the ratio similarly shot up.) The steep climb in 1990s–2000s reflects the tech and financial boom; the subsequent dip in 2008–09 was brief, with the ratio rebounding above 300× in 2020[5].

By contrast, aggregate inequality measures show broader trends. In Piketty’s data, the U.S. top 0.1% income share roughly doubled from ~2% in the 1970s to ~4% by 2010 (and even higher since), while the top 1% rose from ~10% to ~20%. These rises coincide with the surge in CEO-style compensation. International comparisons likewise indicate that countries with lax governance tend to have higher CEO pay: La Porta et al. find weaker shareholder protections (e.g. French civil-law regimes) correlate with fatter CEO packages.

While more formal statistical analysis is needed, the qualitative correlation is clear: periods and places of lax control on executives see sharper jumps in top shares. For example, OECD reports and academic studies note that the U.S. and UK – with highly dispersed ownership and weak shareholder activism – saw the largest pay-surges, whereas countries like Germany and Japan (with stronger stakeholder influence and board oversight) saw more modest increases. Indeed, Piketty observes that Britain’s inequality rise after 1980 was “only about half” that of the U.S.[29], consistent with differences in governance norms.

Mechanisms: How Boards and Norms Drive Self-Perpetuating Pay Link to heading

The engine behind rising CEO pay is a circular pay-setting mechanism. Key elements include (a) boards/compensation committees, (b) executive search/consultant influences, (c) peer benchmarking practices, and (d) social norms around “the right” pay for star CEOs. A simplified flowchart is:

graph LR
    A[Boards & Compensation Committees] -->|Use peer benchmarking| B(Pay Consultants)
    B -->|Suggest 50th–75th percentile| C[CEO Pay Package]
    C -->|Beats median → pressures peers up| D[Peer CEO Pay Medians]
    D -->|Feedback into| C
    C -->|Inflated CEO pay→| E[Executive “Rents”↑]
    E -.->|Undetected by outsiders| A
  • Peer Benchmarking: Boards typically set CEO pay relative to peers. If a CEO is below the peer-group median, he will “merit” a big increase; if above, relatively less. This dynamic guarantees upward pressure. Crucially, when one firm grants a high pay, that moves the median up for all others. Thus pay “ratchets” in a co-operative spiral – a pattern documented by Bizjack et al.[6].
  • Compensation Consultants: Independent firms often advise on pay structures. However, consultants are hired (and paid) by management, creating a conflict: they may recommend aggressive stock-based packages to satisfy CEOs, boosting their own repeat business. In practice, boards frequently follow such advice. (In fact, boards often tout “consultant reports” to justify large packages.) This reinforces peer effects because consultants rely on market surveys of peer pay.
  • Board Composition: In many large firms, CEOs handpick board members or serve alongside fellow executives on each other’s boards. This “social network” effect can weaken independent judgment. Bebchuk & Fried argue that with dispersed owners, these interlocked relationships allow “executive rents” to be hidden as vaguely-defined bonuses or perks[7]. Boards rarely enforce strict clawbacks or question pay beyond customary benchmarking, especially when the board’s own status is tied to keeping the CEO content.
  • Norms and Entrenchment: A long era of rising CEO pay has normalized very large packages. Pay levels become a reference point, and it is politically difficult for any one firm to cut its CEO’s compensation drastically for fear of losing talent. Piketty notes that outrage (“warmongering” around high pay) is often muted if insiders see themselves as deserving “superstar” status[2][30]. This low “outrage cost” lets firms continue a pattern of generous raises.

These mechanisms create a vicious circle: CEOs, once starting from an already high base, press the board (through performance or simply through norms) for more pay; the board cites peer data; consultants validate it. Over decades, this process can elevate CEO pay far beyond productive contribution – exactly the self-reinforcing dynamic Piketty alludes to when managers “set their own pay” unchecked[3][7].

Counterarguments and Alternative Views Link to heading

While managerial power is central, critics cite other factors. Skill or “market for talent” arguments claim that cutting-edge CEOs generate enormous value (especially in tech or finance) and thus command higher pay. As noted, Gabaix & Landier’s superstar model suggests CEO pay ∝ firm value[14]. By this view, globalization and technology have raised the “scale” of firms (and markets) so top executive productivity (and pay) scales too. Similarly, some economists point to economies of scale: a small edge in leadership at a global firm yields massive extra revenue, potentially justifying outsized compensation.

Empirical evidence for skill-based explanations is mixed. On one hand, industries with fast growth (tech, pharma) do pay their CEOs richly. But many high-paying sectors are mature (e.g. oil, consumer goods) where CEOs’ direct impact on short-run profits is debatable. Moreover, if CEOs truly became so much more skilled, we would expect broad-based indicators of skill (like formal education or experience) to correlate tightly with pay; yet data show education levels in top CEOs have plateaued. Gabaix & Landier themselves note the proliferation of MBAs and corporate exec training suggests skill scarcity hasn’t drastically changed[14].

Globalization and shareholder influence: Another view is that international competition and the rise of shareholder activists drive pay. Proponents argue that global labor markets for CEOs force U.S. firms to keep up with, say, European or Asian pay trends. In truth, U.S. CEO pay has long outstripped that abroad, even adjusting for firm size. And cross-country data (e.g. La Porta et al.) usually show exactly the opposite: countries with stronger investor protections (Anglo systems) have higher CEO pay, because boards operate freer from controlling owner constraints. In fact, Europe has seen rising CEO pay only after deregulation and political pressure: e.g. the U.K. and Swiss firms now pay U.S.-like levels, and the 2025 Reuters survey noted growing “say-on-pay” rebellions in Europe[26][11].

“Superstar firms” hypothesis: Recent research by Autor et al. (2017) suggests that a few mega-firms (GAFA tech companies, etc.) capture large market shares and pay top executives correspondingly. This can explain rising concentration of profits and wages at the very top without invoking rent extraction. There is some evidence for this: the five largest U.S. firms now account for a significant portion of profits, and their CEOs (Sundar Pichai, etc.) earn lavish stock packages. However, again the magnitude is extreme: a handful of “superstar CEOs” cannot alone account for the broad surge in median CEO pay across hundreds of companies. In practice, even firms outside the superstar category benchmark themselves against those giants.

In summary, skill- and market-based explanations offer some insight but cannot fully account for the explosive rise in CEO pay relative to other high incomes. As EPI puts it, “CEOs are getting more because of their power to set pay and because so much of their pay is stock-related, not because they are increasing their productivity or possess specific, high-demand skills”[31]. The evidence – e.g. CEO pay >6× top 0.1% pay[4], ratcheting patterns in boards’ decisions[6], and corporate scandals – supports the view that governance weakness and rent-seeking are key drivers.

Policy Recommendations and Trade-offs Link to heading

Given these findings, a combination of policies is proposed to curb self-awarded pay:

  • Higher Top Taxes: Reinstating steep marginal tax rates on high incomes would directly lessen executives’ after-tax gains. Piketty himself advocates progressive taxation as central to restraining rentier income. Economist Josh Bivens suggests adding tax brackets (e.g. at $1m, $5m, $20m) so each additional dollar of CEO pay faces a higher rate[16]. This is straightforward and has historical precedent (mid-20th-century top rates exceeded 90%). The drawback is tax avoidance: highly compensated individuals might shift income to capital or abroad. (Combining with higher capital gains/tax credits on stock awards can mitigate avoidance.)

  • Corporate Excise Taxes: Bivens also outlines targeted corporate taxes on “excess” CEO pay[32]. For example, an excise tax could charge the firm $1 for every $1 that its CEO’s pay exceeds a threshold (say $1m). This penalizes boards directly for overpaying managers. Designing the base is tricky – firms can manipulate reported pay or game worker pay data – but focusing on economy-wide median worker pay as the comparator (instead of the firm’s own median) makes it harder to evade[33]. The advantage is it hits the pocketbook of shareholders indirectly (via higher corporate taxes), giving them an incentive to restrain pay. The trade-off: companies may restructure remuneration (e.g. through off-book perks) to avoid the tax.

  • Disclosure and “Say-on-Pay”: Mandating transparency can help public and investors apply pressure. Rules like the U.S. SEC’s CEO–worker pay ratio disclosure (since 2018) raise awareness of pay gaps, though on their own they don’t compel change. More forceful is enhancing “say-on-pay” votes. Currently U.S. shareholder votes are advisory. One proposal (adopted in some US states and by activist groups) is to make them binding or to penalize directors if votes are opposed[18]. In the UK/EU, “binding votes” on future pay policies are more common; early data from Europe (e.g. Reuters’s 2025 report) show these have started to curb the worst excesses[26][11]. Nonetheless, even binding votes have limits if large institutional holders (like mutual funds) abstain or vote with management.

  • Board/Governance Reform: Strengthening boards is crucial. For instance, requiring truly independent compensation committees (no CEO’s former subordinates) and limiting interlocking directorates can improve checks. Some advocate mandatory tenure limits for execs on boards. Reforms could also mandate that board members themselves face consequences for unpopular pay votes (loss of their own pay or seats) as Bivens suggests[18]. Legal changes could impose clawback provisions beyond financial restatements (e.g. for ethical breaches), to discourage reckless behavior done for a payday. The trade-offs here involve corporate law complexities: too-strict rules risk dissuading qualified people from board service or pushing firms to take in more friendly (but less independent) directors.

  • Labor and Shareholder Empowerment: Finally, empowering other stakeholders can create balance. For example, unionization tends to compress wage disparities and increase executive oversight. Workers on corporate boards (as in Germany/France models) or codetermination could impose additional constraints on pay. Increased use of shareholder proposals (like proxy access, or mandatory vote on pay packages) gives activist investors a stronger voice. The drawback is these changes often face political resistance and require long timelines.

In assessing these options, it’s clear no single fix suffices. Piketty would likely endorse higher progressive taxes as the systemic tool, but also recognize the need for corporate reforms (he notes the “political dimension” of inequality). In practice, a mix is needed: for example, a higher top tax rate would deter runaway pay, while say-on-pay and disclosure mobilize shareholder pressure. Excise taxes specifically targeting pay gaps (as proposed in the U.S. Senate “Tax Excessive CEO Pay Act”[34]) could directly penalize outliers. Clawbacks and stricter governance codes ensure that overt rent-grabs (fraudulent bonuses, golden parachutes after failure) are harder to consummate. Each measure has trade-offs in complexity and enforceability, but together they can alter the incentive structure that Piketty identifies as central to modern inequality.
