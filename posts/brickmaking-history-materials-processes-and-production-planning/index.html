Brickmaking: History, Materials, Processes, and Production Planning

April 4, 2026 23-minute read

Executive Summary Link to heading

  • Ancient origins to industrial era: Humans have made bricks for millennia (sun-dried mud in Mesopotamia, 7000 BC; first fired bricks ~3000 BC) up through large-scale industrialization in the 19th–20th centuries. The continuous Hoffmann kiln (patented 1858[1]) and mechanized presses/extruders revolutionized production, enabling uniform high-temperature firing and much greater efficiency.
  • Raw materials: Bricks are mainly clay/shale with silica and alumina (typical brick clay ~45–55% SiO₂, ~20–30% Al₂O₃[2]). Impurities (excess CaO, organics, sulfates) must be minimized to avoid lime pop, bloating or efflorescence. Particle-size control and plasticity (Atterberg limits) are key raw-material tests.
  • Formulations & testing: Body recipes vary (100% clay to clay+shale+grog or sand). Lab tests measure workability (moisture, plasticity) and fired properties: compressive strength, water absorption, modulus, etc. Most structural clay bricks far exceed minimum standards[3] (typical fired facing bricks average ~100 MPa[4] vs. ASTM minimum ~17 MPa). Compression (ASTM C109/C67), water absorption, and durability tests guide optimization.
  • Forming methods: Three main forming methods are used: stiff-mud extrusion (clay ~12–18% water, vacuum-deaired and wire-cut to shape)[5]; soft-mud molding (20–30% water, pressed in sand or steel molds)[6]; and dry-pressing (4–6% moisture, high-pressure pressing in dies). Stiff-mud extrusion dominates modern brickmaking (≈80–90% in industrial plants)[5] because it provides homogeneous plastic flow and easy automation.
  • Equipment: Bench-scale production uses small vacuum pugmills (e.g. Peter Pugger), hand- or foot-presses, and small drying ovens. Industrial lines use continuous pugmills/extruders (capacity tons/hour), high-capacity vacuum systems, wire cutters, hydraulic presses, tunnel kilns, and automated conveyors. For example, a startup shaping line (grinder + pugmill + extruder) costing ~$1.35 M can produce 50,000 bricks/day[7], paired with a tunnel kiln ($2.5 M) for that scale[8]. Table 1 (below) compares small, medium, and large operation scales.
  • Process flow & control: Raw clay is quarried, crushed/granulated, then mixed with water (and grog/sand as needed) in pugmills. The blend is tempered (rested) to homogenize. Forming (extrude/press), then drying (to ~2–3% moisture) and firing follow. Critical control points include moisture content at extrusion, drying rate (to prevent cracks), and kiln temperature profile. See flowchart below for a typical process.
  • Drying & firing: Green bricks are dried in chamber or tunnel dryers (up to ~200 °C). Most firing is done in continuous tunnel kilns (~100–150 m long, firing zone ~1,090 °C[9]). Single batch periodic kilns are also used in smaller plants. Typical firing cycles last 20–50 hours[10]. Fuel is usually natural gas (some coal, biomass, or oil as backup). Figure: small-scale clamp kilns (sun-dried bricks and open firing) are still common in some countries【73†】.
  • Quality standards: In the U.S. and Canada, ASTM C67 governs brick testing (sampling, compressive strength, absorption, freeze-thaw, efflorescence, warpage, etc.)[11]. Other relevant standards include ASTM C62/C216 (solid bricks), C652 (hollow brick), and ISO EN methods. Key QC tests are compressive strength (ASTM C109), cold water absorption (ASTM C67), and freeze-thaw durability. Bricks are often classified (e.g. “SW” grade freeze-resistant). Comprehensive QA programs sample from each kiln load.
  • Failure modes: Common defects guide process tuning. Under-fired bricks (insufficient peak temperature or soak) show low strength and high absorption[12]. Bloating (spongy swollen appearance) comes from organics or sulfates in clay[13]. Efflorescence (white salt deposits) comes from soluble alkali salts in clay or water uptake[14]. Lime popping is caused by free CaO/CaCO₃, which absorb moisture and “blow” the brick[15]. Cracking and warping occur if drying or firing is too rapid or uneven[16]. Controlling raw chemistry and adjusting drying/firing ramps mitigate these.
  • Porosity & microstructure: Fired bricks are a porous ceramic: total porosity and pore-size distribution strongly influence strength and durability. Denser vitrified bricks (low porosity) have higher compressive strength[17]. However, a small fraction of larger pores can improve freeze-thaw resistance (e.g. Maage factor metric)[18]. Advanced characterization (mercury porosimetry, SEM, X-ray CT) can quantify pore sizes. In general, bricks for mechanical robustness aim for minimal pore volume and uniform fine pores (low absorption). Moisture in pores lowers strength and enables freeze damage[19].
  • Example recipes: Typical clay-brick mixes might be ~100% clay or clay+10–20% shale/grog/sand. Table 2 (below) summarizes example brick types: ordinary building brick, face/facing brick, heavy engineering brick, and fly-ash geopolymer brick. For instance, an engineering brick might fire at >1,150 °C to achieve >100 MPa compressive strength and <5% absorption, whereas a common brick at ~1,050 °C yields ~20–50 MPa and ~10% absorption. Geopolymer bricks (fly ash + alkali binder) can reach 10–30 MPa without firing.
  • Environmental & regulatory: Brick kilns emit CO₂ (from fuel and clay), NOₓ, SOₓ (if coal/sulfur), particulates, HCl/HF (from clays), and volatile organics[20]. The U.S. NESHAP “Brick and Clay” rule regulates hazardous air pollutants (mercury, metals, acids) from brick kilns[21]. Water use is modest (for mixing), but wastewater (from cleaning) and solid waste (scrap bricks, kiln draw) must be managed. Modern trends use biomass or solar preheating, fly ash/slag additives, and waste-heat recovery to cut fuel and raw use[22][23]. For example, Wienerberger is testing hydrogen-fired kilns (2027) to cut >9% of site CO₂ without quality loss[23]. Overall, life-cycle impacts are driving low-carbon materials (e.g. unfired blocks, geopolymers)[24].
  • Economics (CAPEX/OPEX): Scale dictates cost. A small artisanal setup (1,000–5,000 bricks/day) might use a single extruder or hand-press, a rack dryer or clamp kiln, and capital of $\sim$10–100k. A medium plant (~50k bricks/day) with continuous extrusion, pugmill, shaker, dryer, and a tunnel kiln might be \5–10M (for equipment and shed)[7][8]. A large plant (100k+/day) with multiple lines and kilns could be \20M+. Table 3 (below) compares scales: equipment lists, throughput, staff, and ballpark costs (CAPEX/OPEX). Utilities (energy, fuel) typically dominate OPEX (30–40% of cost)[25], with raw clay ~25–35% and labor the rest.
  • Safety & permitting: Key hazards include kiln fire/heat, dust (silica), and heavy equipment. Permitting covers air (emissions limits), wastewater, and zoning. Brick dust and kiln heat must be controlled (ventilation, filters, PPE). Workplace safety requires training on forklifts, kiln operations, and material handling.
  • Supply chain: Clay/shale is bulky – proximity to a clay quarry is critical. Raw materials must be tested (XRF, sieve, Atterberg tests) before plant design. Additives like sand, grog, or fly ash (if used) must be sourced and blended reliably. Byproducts (washoff, scrap bricks) should be recycled on-site (e.g. recycled into grog). Local regulations on mining and transport (truck emissions) apply.
  • R&D & optimization: A systematic R&D plan is recommended: lab test raw clays (mineralogy, plasticity), trial bodies (batch pugmills/extruders), and small-kiln firing experiments. Use Design of Experiments (DOE) to vary water content, clay blend, and firing curve. Characterize samples (ASTM C67 tests, porosimetry, SEM). Pilot-scale runs (e.g. 100–1,000 bricks) validate performance before scale-up. Figure below shows a 18–24 month R&D Gantt plan (materials testing → pilot extrusion → kiln trials → optimization). This structured approach de-risks plant startup and ensures product targets (strength, durability, etc.) are met.
  • Future trends: The brick industry is moving toward sustainability and automation. Examples include unfired block presses (hydraulically compressed fly-ash or clay blocks, eliminating kilns), geopolymer bricks (no firing), and 3D-printed masonry[26]. Advanced materials (foamed insulative bricks, fiber-reinforced composites) are emerging for niche applications. Electrified/hydrogen kilns (to cut fossil fuel) are being piloted[23]. Data-driven process control (AI-monitored kilns, real-time moisture sensors) is also advancing. Adopting these can future-proof a brick plant while meeting stricter CO₂ and quality regulations[24].

History and Lineage Link to heading

Brickmaking is one of the oldest technologies. Ancient communities used sun-dried mud bricks (e.g. 7000 BC in Mesopotamia), then developed simple firing (e.g. Egyptian and Indus civilizations by 3000 BC). Medieval Europe standardized brick sizes and styles (e.g. Gothic brick cathedrals). The first continuous (ring) kiln was patented by Friedrich Hoffmann in 1858[1], enabling continuous high-temperature firing and mass production. In the late 19th/early 20th century, mechanization (gravity-fed mixers, vacuum pugmills, extrusion presses) allowed further scale-up. By the 1920s–50s tunnel kilns (long continuous tunnels) replaced many intermittent kilns. In recent decades, computer control, emission regulations, and energy efficiency have driven new innovations (Figure below).

Raw Materials Link to heading

Clays and shales (sedimentary mudstones) are the primary inputs. A typical brick clay contains ~45–55% silica (SiO₂), ~20–30% alumina (Al₂O₃), with Fe₂O₃ giving color and smaller quantities of Na₂O, K₂O, MgO, etc[2]. Low CaO is preferable (<5% CaO) to avoid expansion defects. Mineralogy (kaolinite, illite, etc.) and chemistry determine shrinkage, firing color, and sintering behavior. Impurities: Organic matter (oxidizes to gas, causes bloating), sulfates (can cause black core or speckling), chlorides/nitrates (efflorescence). These must be tested (XRF, LOI, chemical analysis) and often reduced by washing or selective blending. Particle size: Clay is often ground (to ≤150 µm) and blended with sand or grog (pre-fired crushed brick) to control shrinkage. Plasticity (measured by Atterberg limits) and liquid/plastic limit define workable moisture ranges. Additives like barium carbonate are sometimes mixed in to bind sulfur and prevent surface specks[27].

Body Formulation and Testing Link to heading

Brickbody recipes vary by product. A simple recipe might be “100 parts clay, 10 parts sand” (for a coarse brick) or “80 parts clay, 20 parts shale” (for a finer brick). Each batch is tested for moisture content and plasticity. Key lab tests:

  • Atterberg limits and plasticity index to ensure the clay can mold without cracking.
  • Proctor test (compaction) to estimate density at given moisture.
  • Shrinkage tests (measure linear shrinkage on drying and firing).
  • Fired property tests: Standard test bricks are formed (by hand or small press), dried and fired to profile, then tested. Compressive strength is measured (ASTM C109 in a hydraulic press). Water absorption is measured by boiling test (ASTM C67 procedure). Durability tests: cold water saturation (C67), freeze-thaw cycles, efflorescence rating (C67 methods). Target properties depend on brick type: e.g. a facing brick might aim for ≥50 MPa and <10% absorption, while an engineering brick aims for >100 MPa and <4% absorption. In general, increased firing temperature or soak time reduces porosity and increases strength[17]. The Brick Industry Association notes that most commercial bricks achieve compressive strengths far above ASTM minima[3]. For example, one survey found average fired facing brick ~100 MPa[4]. Data sheets/standards guide formulation; iterative adjustment of water content, raw mix, and firing is used to hit targets.

Forming Methods Link to heading

Bricks can be formed in three principal ways[28][6]:

  • Stiff-mud (extrusion): The most common method (~80–90% of bricks)[5]. Clay (typically 12–18% water by weight) is mixed and passed through a vacuum pugmill to de-air and homogenize, then extruded through a rectangular die. The clay “green” column is cut to length by a moving wire. This yields smooth-surfaced bricks (for facing brick) or more textured (by sand coating). Extrusion is continuous and easily automated. Vacuum de-airing eliminates air pockets, producing a homogeneous mass.
  • Soft-mud molding: Higher moisture (20–30%). The clay is pressed into steel or sand molds (hand-formed or machine-molded). Used for some ornamental or heritage-style bricks. Yields more uneven texture; often requires sand on mold to release. Labor-intensive and less uniform.
  • Dry-pressing: Very low moisture (~5%). Powdered clay/shale is poured into steel molds and pressed under high pressure (up to 80–150 MPa). Produces very dense, precise bricks (often pavers or engineering brick). Equipment is batch (punch or compression molding). Few dusts, but requires extremely tight moisture control and high force.

Equipment and Scale Link to heading

Bench-scale (Pilot/Artisanal): Link to heading

  • Pugmills: Small vacuum clay mixers (e.g. studio pugmill, 100–500 kg batch) for mixing and de-airing. Example: Peter Pugger VPM-60 (3 hp, ~150 kg/h capacity).
  • Extruders/Presses: Table-top extruders (manual or small motor-driven) or hand-presses. These produce a few hundred bricks/day.
  • Kiln/Dryer: Electric or gas-fired muffle kiln (for small batches up to ~100 bricks), or even a homemade clamp kiln (earthen stack).
  • Curing: Sun-drying on racks or ground (as in Figures).
    This scale requires minimal automation and 1–3 operators. CAPEX ~$5k–$50k (equipment plus small shed). OPEX is low but labor and manual handling are high.

Medium to Large Industrial Scale: Link to heading

  • Material prep: Crushers, ball mills or hammer mills to grind raw clay; large storage silos for clay and sand.
  • Mixers: Continuous pugmills (twin-screw extruders or paddle mixers, 1–10 t/hr). Vacuum chamber integrated for de-airing.
  • Extruders & Wire cutters: Large-capacity extruder (e.g. 100–1,000+ kg/h). Automatic wire cutters and clay conveyors.
  • Hydraulic presses (if used): Multiple-station presses (up to thousands of psi) for special brick/paver lines.
  • Dryers: Tunnel or chamber dryers, 50–200 m long, with controlled hot-air flow and humidity.
  • Kilns: Tunnel kilns (continuous) or Hoffman ring kilns for 100+ tons/day; downdraft periodic kilns for moderate capacity. Kilns often ~100–150 m long, with preheat, firing, and cooling zones. Key instruments: thermocouples in zones, oxygen/fuel controls.
  • Handling: Brick conveyors, robotic stackers, forklifts, and cranes. Data acquisition for moisture and temperature.
    At large scale (>20 M bricks/year), plants have >50 workers and hundreds of millions in revenue.

Drying and Firing Engineering Link to heading

After shaping, green bricks must be dried (typically to ~2–3% moisture) before firing to avoid steam-blasting. Drying is done in chambers or tunnel dryers with gradual heating (usually <200 °C). Inexpensive setups simply dry bricks in the open air for 1–2 days, then fire in an improvised stack. Larger plants use heated, insulated dryers (hot air at ~100–204 °C) often powered by waste kiln heat[9]. Drying schedule is critical: too fast causes cracks, too slow wastes capacity. Typical drying takes 8–24 hours.

Most industrial kilns are continuous tunnel kilns: long brick-lined tunnels (100–150 m) on rails where cars of bricks move slowly through zones. Figure [73] (below) shows a traditional brickfield with clamp-kilns and drying rows. In the tunnel kiln, bricks are preheated, then a firing zone (~1,090 °C) for sintering[9], then cooled. Natural gas is the usual fuel (some plants use coal or biomass). Total kiln cycle is ~20–50 hours[10]. Modern kilns recover heat: exhaust gases are passed through cooler zones and pre-heaters before venting. Clamp kilns (as in Figures) are simple open structures where bricks are stacked with fuel; these are cheap but inefficient and polluting. In open clamps, bricks often sun-dry on the ground (foreground in Fig.【73†】) before being fired under bellows or chimney.

Tunnel kilns are far more efficient: waste heat recovery means flue gases exit ~100–150 °C (vs. 800+ °C for simple kilns)[29]. Table 2 compares kiln types (e.g. tunnel vs. periodic). Operating kilns requires precise control: ramp rates (for moisture evaporation, dehydroxylation, vitrification) and soak times determine final microstructure. Control systems monitor zone temperatures and oxygen; occasional fuel “flashing” (reducing atmosphere) is used to adjust brick color[30].

*Figure: Outdoor brickfield with traditional clamp firing (tall chimneys) and drying yard (foreground rows of sun-dried bricks)【73†】.*

Kiln firing process: Evaporate free water (up to 100 °C), dehydrate clays (~100–600 °C), burn out organics (~300–600 °C), oxidize impurities, then sinter/vitrify (~900–1100 °C). The firing zone reaches ~1090 °C[31]. After firing, bricks cool gradually. Control of temperature and time is essential: overshoot can cause warping or melt, undershoot gives weak bricks.

Fuel and heat: Natural gas is most common. Coal or biomass (e.g. rice husks) are used in some regions. Oil furnaces or LPG are backups. Advances include electrified tunnel kilns (electric heaters or induction) and pilot projects for 100% hydrogen firing[23] to eliminate fossil CO₂.

Quality Control & Standards Link to heading

Quality is ensured through standardized testing and inspection. In North America, ASTM C67 is the main test standard for brick and tile[11]. It covers sampling, dimensional checks, compressive strength (via ASTM C109), water absorption, cold water saturation coefficient, freeze–thaw durability, efflorescence, warpage, and dimensions[11]. For building codes, specific standards apply: e.g. ASTM C62 (Building Brick), C216 (Facing Brick), C902 (Solid Masonry Pavers), C652 (Hollow Brick), each with strength and absorption limits. Bricks are also graded (e.g. Grade N/S/W in ASTM) for non-load, severe freeze, etc. European/ISO standards (EN 771-1) similarly classify bricks by density, strength, and water absorption.

Typical sampling: one test brick is drawn from each kiln car or every few thousand bricks. Compressive strength is often much higher than the ASTM minimum (2.5 ksi or 17 MPa)[3]. Water absorption (vacuum saturation) target depends on use; <20% is common for face bricks, <12% for pavers, <8% for engineering bricks. Freeze-thaw is checked by repeated freezing cycles (usually 20 cycles), with allowable damage in millimeters. Efflorescence is scored visually (ASTM C67 suggests “none, slight, moderate”).

Microtesting: Advanced plants may use CT scanning or microscopy to examine cracks/pores. Routine QC may include an on-site hardness (sclerometer) or ultrasonic velocity as quick checks of brick density.

Failure Modes and Diagnostics Link to heading

Understanding defects is key to troubleshooting[12][13]:

  • Underfiring: Bricks fired at too low peak temperature or insufficient soak. Result: low strength, high absorption, pale color. (Troubleshoot by increasing kiln temperature/time). [12].
  • Bloating (“overburn”): Surface bubbles or spongy texture from gases (carbonaceous or sulfide) trapped in the brick. Often caused by high sulfur/organic clay with too-rapid firing[13]. Solutions include pre-drying more thoroughly, slower firing ramp, or adding barium carbonate to bind sulfur.
  • Black core: Incomplete burnout of organics (reducing core) can leave carbon or iron sulphide streaks.
  • Cracking: Straight cracks (through bricks) often from rapid drying or firing. Random cracks (inexplicable) may be due to pebble/rock in clay or non-uniform pressing. Address by slowing the cycle or improving clay processing. [16].
  • Warping/Dishing: Bricks that curve or have high edges. Caused by uneven moisture content across brick face or uneven firing. Optimize drainage and airflow in the kiln.
  • Efflorescence: White salt crystals on surface after wetting. Caused by soluble salts (chlorides, sulfates) in clay. Mitigate by washing clay or using salt-free additions; post-treat bricks (e.g. sealer).[14].
  • Spalling/Pop-outs: Caused by rapid heating of absorbed water (steam expansion) or by expansion of internal CaO. Slower heating or longer pre-dry solves it.
  • Dimensional errors: Over-size bricks often mean under-firing (insufficient shrinkage); under-size can indicate over-burning or measurement variation.

For each defect, cause is traced by analyzing raw material chemistry and reviewing each process step (mix, press/extrude pressure, drying uniformity, kiln profile). Non-destructive tests (e.g. X-ray, ultrasound) can detect internal voids before firing.

Porosity and Microstructure Link to heading

Bricks are a porous ceramic with pore volumes typically 15–25%. Pore size and distribution govern performance: fine pores hold water and increase capillarity; large pores (often from burned-out organics or voids) can act as stress concentrators but help relieve frost damage. Mercury Intrusion Porosimetry (MIP) is often used to quantify pore-size distribution; Micro-CT or SEM imaging reveal pore networks. A lower total porosity generally means higher compressive strength[17]. However, very low porosity (<5%) can make bricks too brittle for freeze-thaw. As one study notes, freeze–thaw resistance correlates not only with total porosity but with proportion of larger pores (allowing ice expansion)[18]. Thus a robust brick balances moderate porosity (for durability) with strong clay bonding. Relation to strength: Numerous data show water absorption inversely correlates with strength – e.g. typical face bricks (~10% absorption) have much higher strength than porous AAC blocks (~20–50 MPa vs. ~5 MPa). Reducing clay impurities and increasing sintering densifies the matrix, raising modulus and strength[17].

Environmental, Regulatory, and Emissions Link to heading

Brick plants are energy- and emission-intensive. Fuel use (for firing ~1000 °C) dominates energy: typical tunnel kilns use on the order of 0.8–1.5 MJ/kg of fired brick[32] (roughly 0.25–0.40 kWh/kg). This translates to significant CO₂: burning gas releases ~2.75 kg CO₂ per cubic meter, so a small plant can emit tens of thousands of tons CO₂/year. Pollutants: Particulate matter (PM₁₀/PM₂.₅) from raw materials and kilns, NOₓ and CO from combustion, SO₂ (if fuel or clay contains sulfur), HCl/HF (if clays contain chlorides/fluorides)[20]. Hazardous air pollutants (HAPs) like mercury and metals in clay are also regulated. The U.S. EPA’s NESHAP (Subpart JJJJJ) specifically covers brick/structural clay products, limiting mercury, HAP, and PM emissions[21]. Dryers (if heated by burner) can emit NOₓ/CO, so many dryers use only kiln waste heat.

Plants also manage waste water (from cleaning extruders/molds) and solid waste (dust, damaged bricks). Many operations recycle kiln dust as filler (grog). Best practices: scrubbers or electrostatic precipitators capture kiln dust/PM (requiring 95% removal for SOₓ scrubbers in some systems[33]). Carbon reduction: as noted above, initiatives include switching to biomass or hydrogen fuels[23], recovering flue heat for drying, and using industrial wastes as feedstock (fly ash, slag, even brick dust). A recent industry guide emphasizes unfired block machines (no kiln) and geopolymers (cement-free binders) as low-emission alternatives[24]. Environmental audits, stack testing, and life-cycle analyses (LCAs) are recommended for any new plant.

Safety, Permitting, and Supply Chain Link to heading

Safety: Hazard controls include dust suppression (silica dust), high-temperature guarding (kiln fires), pinch points (extruder), and manual handling. Adequate ventilation and PPE are mandatory. Hot zones and moving machinery (conveyors, forklifts) require barriers and training. Fire safety is critical around kilns (gas/oil lines, refractory integrity).
Permits: Typical permits cover air emissions (PM, VOCs, NOₓ), wastewater discharge, solid waste handling, and land use. Food/compostable requirements are negligible, but OSHA or local machine-safety codes apply. For environmental compliance, a MACT/NESHAP permit (US) or equivalent must be obtained for kilns, often requiring emission monitoring.
Supply chain: Clay usually must be quarried or delivered from a nearby claypit (transport cost is high due to weight). Blending of clays (to achieve consistent mix) is common. Suppliers of sand, grog, fly ash (if used), and fuels should be vetted for consistency. Seasonal variations (wet vs dry clay) can affect moisture content; an on-site QC lab or quick tests are helpful. Quality control of incoming raw materials (XRF chemical, sieve analysis, Methylene Blue test for clay) prevents surprises.

Key tasks:

  1. Months 1–6: Test raw clays (XRD, XRF) and blends. Perform Atterberg/plasticity tests. Develop small “recipe” batches (vary clay/stone ratio, moisture). Use a laboratory pugmill and small press or mold to make specimens. Dry (room/oven) and fire (laboratory kiln at several profiles). Measure shrinkage, density, compressive strength, absorption. Select promising mixes.
  2. Months 7–12: Conduct pilot extrusion trials: equip a small vacuum pugmill/extruder to form bricks. Establish target moisture and extrusion pressure. Standardize drying curve (e.g. 8h at 80 °C plus 100 °C). Begin firing in a pilot kiln (brick car or small tunnel if available). Adjust firing schedule (ramp rate, soak time) to minimize warpage and achieve desired color/strength.
  3. Months 13–18: Test durability: do ASTM C67 strength/absorption, freeze-thaw, efflorescence on pilot bricks. Refine mix and process as needed (e.g. add grog, adjust particle size). Scale up batch size gradually. Analyze data and iterate DOE as needed.
  4. Months 19–24: Prepare for scale-up: procure and commission equipment (pugmill/extruder, molds, kiln). Run a pilot production (e.g. 10k bricks) with full process. Finalize QA protocols and sampling plans. Document standard operating procedures. At end, have a validated process meeting all target specs.

This schedule ensures a structured development, minimizing trial-and-error delays. Regular cross-functional reviews (materials, process engineers, quality) should guide go/no-go decisions at each phase.

Future Trends Link to heading

The brick industry is innovating rapidly around sustainability and automation. Unfired technologies – hydraulic block presses that compress fly ash, lime, or cement – can eliminate kilns altogether (zero firing CO₂). Geopolymers (fly ash/slag activated by alkalis) are being formulated into extrudable “bricks” that cure at ambient or moderate heat[26], drastically cutting emissions. For example, 3D-printed foamed geopolymer blocks (using recycled brick/AAC waste) have been demonstrated, offering good insulation and ~5–7 MPa strength[34]. Automation/AI: Vision-based defect inspection and AI kiln controls are emerging to optimize quality and reduce labor. Some plants use robots to stack bricks and automated guided vehicles (AGVs) for material movement. Low-carbon firing: Major producers are piloting hydrogen- or electric-fired kilns. Wienerberger plans the world’s first hydrogen brick kiln by 2027[23]. Electricity (from renewables) can power coil or induction heating to 1000+ °C. Over time, these shifts could decarbonize brickmaking while maintaining product performance[23][24]. Finally, new brick formats (3D-printed interlocking masonry, gas-filled lightweight bricks, fiber-reinforced composites) are on the horizon, potentially expanding bricks’ applications. Brick plant design should thus remain flexible to adopt such advances.
